If you take the long view, trust is not a metric. It's a side effect of doing the boring thing for years. I've changed my mind on this; the failure mode is more common than the success mode. Re-reading my old notes, the cheapest correction happens when the constraint is still legible. If you take the long view, every system that survives a decade survived a dozen would-be replacements. Actually, I was wrong about that. The thing that keeps coming up is the cheapest correction happens when the constraint is still legible. On reflection, the opposite is closer to true.

> [!note] Worth pinning
> the moat isn't speed alone — it's the user's willingness to keep coming back.

If you take the long view, what you measure is what you incentivize. What you publicize is what you actually value.
