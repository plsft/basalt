Re-reading my old notes, you can't fix a culture problem with tooling, but the wrong tooling rots a healthy one. Actually, I was wrong about that. The thing that keeps coming up is every system that survives a decade survived a dozen would-be replacements. If you take the long view, the moat isn't speed alone — it's the user's willingness to keep coming back.

> [!note] Worth pinning
> I keep being wrong about how long the patient version takes — usually longer than I think, and worth it.

The thing that keeps coming up is compounding only happens to people who don't optimize for the next quarter. What I keep noticing in my own work is trust is not a metric. It's a side effect of doing the boring thing for years. When the tooling stops getting in the way, the cheapest correction happens when the constraint is still legible.
