# Reading — James Carse, Finite and Infinite Games (1986)

> **Centerpiece quote:** *"Finite players play within boundaries; infinite players play with them."*

## The frame

A finite game is played to win — bounded objectives, fixed rules, well-defined victory. An infinite game is played to keep playing — the rules and the players themselves shift over time. Carse's claim is that almost everything important in a life is the second kind, even when the participants behave as though it's the first.

## What this connects to

Reading this in 2026, the resonance is with the work I keep gravitating toward — the projects nobody requested, the tools that don't have a clear "v1" target, the experiments that compound only because no quarterly review can grade them. The infinite player isn't avoiding the finite game; they're refusing to let the finite game define what's worth doing.

The strongest framing in the book: *"The asker's imagination is the boundary of the asked-for. Whatever expands the boundary itself was, by definition, unrequested."*

## Why I keep returning to this

It's the cleanest articulation I've seen of why the work that compounds is the work nobody asked for. The book is forty years old; the pattern is older.
