# Reference — Compounding Patterns

> A short essay on the asymmetry between requested and unrequested work.

## The asymmetry

Work that compounds tends to share one property: nobody asked for it. The asker's imagination bounds what they can ask for. The compounding work runs past the asker's imagination by definition — that's why it compounds. The asked-for work plateaus at the asker's expectations.

This connects directly to Carse's *Finite and Infinite Games* — the asked-for work is finite play; the unasked work is infinite play. The book is forty years old and the pattern was old when Carse named it.

## Operational consequence

When deciding what to put on the calendar Monday morning, the test is not *"who is waiting for this?"* but *"would this still matter if nobody was watching?"* The first question selects against compounding work. The second selects for it. Both questions feel productive in the moment; only one actually compounds over a year.

## Pattern instances

- Most meaningful tools at every company I've worked at started as side projects nobody requested.
- The strategic decisions that aged best in retrospect were almost all unannounced at the time.
- The notes that other notes link back to most often are the ones written without a request driving them.

## The trap

This pattern is easy to describe and hard to live. The pull toward asked-for work is constant — it's where the praise lives, where the deadlines land, where the social proof signals are. Resisting that pull requires deliberate practice, not just intellectual assent.
